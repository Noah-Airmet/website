The purpose of this project is to help you to internalize many of the principles and lessons we have learned throughout the semester. To do so, I would like you to create a personal code of ethics. I hope you will take this seriously and use it well beyond this class. The purpose of a personal code of ethics is to help you put into writing those things that you value most and want to commit to. It can be used as document against which you can measure your life and the choices that you make. It can also be used to help others, including future employers, understand your values and mindsets.

The final document should be close to one page of size twelve text, but no more. It should be formatted to look nice – e.g., use spacing and headers to be clear and concise. This is NOT an essay. It is a concise description of your core values. It should be carefully worded to convey the core idea and nothing more in the exact words you choose to use. You may read other codes of ethics for ideas. Indeed, I would hope you would look at many examples for inspiration. However, you should make it your own. Change the words and descriptions to fit your life. Make sure you do not plagiarize by copying things verbatim. If you do copy some parts from elsewhere, make sure you cite things properly (you can put citations on a second page if you’d like).

The format of your personal statement should include a short introduction. This may include a quote that helps define you, a short summary of the person you hope to become and why, a brief description of what this personal code of ethics is and why it exists. I’ll leave it up to you, but plan on having at least 4 sentences introducing the personal code of ethics. You can also call your code of ethics whatever you want. Make it something you would feel comfortable sharing with others. Next, it should include a series of values or value statements and explanations of them. 

Here’s some things that might be useful as you choose values or value statements to include:
1)	Ask yourself: What do I want people to say about me at my funeral? What values do I want to define me? What do I struggle with now that I need to and plan to overcome?
2)	Think about the contexts you anticipate seeing yourself in: family, professional, church…
3)	Draft statements about the values and when needed, incorporate aspects of context into them. 

Your values and value statements may take several forms. It’s up to you. The example below includes many different types of statements related to the value of “excellence” to help give you some ideas.


Excellence: I will only put forward my best work. Part of excellence is continually improving my skills and learning from feedback from others, no matter how it was given. People will have confidence that they can rely upon my work. As a cybersecurity professional, I recognize that excellence is particularly important because it affects the safety and privacy of others. 

Notice this format included (a) a title value (i.e., excellence), (b) an “I will” statement, (c) an explanatory statement, (d) a statement indicating the effect or evidence that I’ve succeeded, and (e) a statement tying it to a particular context (i.e., cybersecurity professional). Your statements don’t need to include all of these elements, but you may consider each of these types of statements, or others you come up with. Typically, you will have several sentences explaining a single value. But those could all be “I will” statements or explanatory statements etc.

Instead of a single value, you may want to have a value statement. For example, the prior example could have used “Strive for Excellence.” Other value statements might be even longer (more like those in the ACM Coder of Ethics, though with a more personal twist). For example: “Seek to improve the lives of others.” This would then be followed by the detailed statements explaining it (as described above).

It’s best not to have too many (e.g., more than 12 seems excessive) or too few (e.g., fewer than 5 seems too few). Focus on ones that are most important to you and then use the descriptive statements (as illustrated above) to flesh them out. If you have fewer values or value statements, then you will need more explanatory text for each one. Thus, the number of words will likely be similar.

Grading
Your grade will be based on the following:
1)	Introduction (1.5 points)
2)	Values (7 points)
3)	Grammar (1.5 points)

The quality of thought, writing, and evidence that you have “made it your own” by customizing it for your own situation in life. You will not be penalized for choosing the “wrong” values. However, you may be penalized for descriptive text that doesn’t match the value, ambiguous statements that can easily be misinterpreted, use of passive voice or poor grammar and typos.
